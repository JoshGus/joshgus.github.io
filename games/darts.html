Hand-Tracking Darts
MediaPipe · fully in-browser · no hardware needed
Camera Setup

Position your webcam so it faces you from the front. Click Camera and choose a preset — Laptop for a screen-top webcam, High if it's mounted well above. Adjust Cam Y until throws land where you aim.

Throwing

Pinch your thumb and index finger together to grip the dart. Then throw forward with a natural arm motion and release the pinch. Throw slightly upward to arc onto the board — flat throws land low.

Physics

Open Gameplay to tune gravity, throw speed, and distance. Sim is the balanced default. Easy makes the dart more forgiving. Pinch sens adjusts how tightly you need to pinch — increase it if throws aren't registering.

Two-Player Game

Click 2-Player Game and both players ready up: P1 holds 1 finger, P2 holds 2 fingers for one second each. Between turns, the incoming player holds an open hand to start their countdown.

Windows / slow performance? Enable Chrome hardware acceleration: Settings → System → Use hardware acceleration when available, then relaunch. Verify at chrome://gpu — WebGL should say Hardware accelerated.

Dev notes

foundation

The premise: fully in-browser, no server

Google's @mediapipe/tasks-vision HandLandmarker runs in WebAssembly, GPU-accelerated, returning 21 landmarks per hand at up to 60 fps with zero backend. The MediaPipe inference runs in a dedicated Web Worker so the 10–15ms WASM calls don't stall the render loop.

The first version was conceptually simple: palm scale (wrist → middle knuckle distance in 2D) grows when your hand moves toward the camera. A sudden scale increase triggered a throw, and landing position was just where the index fingertip was pointing plus random scatter weighted by how jittery your aim had been over the last 12 frames. Steadier hand = tighter grouping.

bug

Darts vanishing with no score and no miss

When I replaced aim-scatter with real throw physics — capturing regression velocity from hand history and launching a ballistic arc — darts started silently disappearing. No score, no miss flash, just gone. Turned out noisy wrist/fingertip regression was producing enormous lateral velocity (vwx or vwy) while the forward velocity was capped at its minimum. The dart exits the side of the screen before frame 2.

Fix: clamp lateral velocity to at most 2× the forward component at all times. A dart physically cannot deviate sideways faster than it's traveling forward.

bug

Sideways swipes triggering throws

The original throw gate used canvasSpeed > 1.0 as a fallback — if the hand moved fast in any direction, throw. A quick lateral swipe across the board would launch a dart. Removed that fallback entirely; only forward velocity (fwdV > 0.3) qualifies, and the forward window (from pullback peak to release) must span at least 50ms to give the regression enough data.

feature

Floor hits: Oops!

With lateral issues fixed, a new pattern emerged: throws that felt right but whose arc crashed into the floor before reaching the board. Added a floor check in the physics tick — if wy < −2.5 while the dart is still in flight, it's a floor hit. In game mode the dart is returned to your hand and "Oops!" flashes on screen. Doesn't count as a throw.

Also added a ~ debug console at this point. Every THROW, OOPS, and SKIP event logs raw velocity data so I could see exactly what was happening with each release.

discovery

Camera resolution was degrading depth accuracy

The camera stream was defaulting to 640×480 @30fps — the original code had hard max caps. MediaPipe is designed for full 720p+ input; fewer pixels per palm landmark means a noisier palm-scale reading, and palm scale is what all depth estimation (and therefore throw physics) depends on. Removed the caps, now requests 1280×720 @60fps as the ideal. Added a Performance panel with Max/Medium/Low presets for weaker hardware.

discovery

The vertical arc problem: regression vwy is structurally too small

Even after all the above, most throws would arc briefly then hit the floor. The debug console revealed the issue: the player's hand spawns at wy0 ≈ −2.8 to −3.2 board-radii (far below the board center in camera space), and gravity adds another ~4 board-radii of downward pull over the ~450ms flight. The vertical velocity needed to reach board center is around 0.015 board-radii/ms.

The regression was giving 0.002–0.006 — roughly 4–7× too small. The reason is fundamental: a dart throw is mostly a depth change, not a vertical one. The wrist moves maybe 0.3px/ms upward in camera space during a throw. That divided by board radius is tiny. No amount of tuning the speed multiplier would fix this without also sending horizontal throws into orbit.

fix

Analytic vertical arc

Decoupled vertical physics from the regression entirely. After all forward/lateral velocity caps are applied, compute the flight time (tLand = −wz0 / vwz), then analytically solve for the vwy that lands the dart at board center: vwyCtr = (−wy0 + ½·G·tLand²) / tLand. The player's regression vwy feeds only a tanh-saturated aim offset of ±1.5 board-radii around that center — steep upward throw aims high, flat or downward throw aims low.

This also meant removing vwy from the 3D speed cap and lateral clamp, since those would immediately crush the analytically computed value back down.

fix

EMA smoothing on landmark coordinates

Throw-to-throw variance was still noisy — individual frames of wrist/fingertip data jitter by a few pixels, which skews regression slopes. Applied exponential moving average (α = 0.4, ~42ms time-constant at 60fps) to all tracked coordinates before they enter the position history. The EMA state runs continuously so it's already settled at the current hand position the moment a new pinch starts. Spawn depth (wz0) uses the median of the last 5 frames instead of a single sample, eliminating single-frame palmSc spikes from distorting the flight time.

fix

Lower board was unreachable

The aim gesture is centered on VWY_NEUTRAL — the raw_vwy value that maps to "aim at center." It was initially set to 0.002, but real throw data showed typical values of 0.002–0.004, meaning the gesture offset was almost always positive and darts always landed above center. Raised to 0.003: typical slight-upward throw now hits center, flatter throws land in the lower half, steeper throws go high.
